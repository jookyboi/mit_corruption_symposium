The US political system is broken

Our government has been bought.
We all know this, but you can change it.

Citizens Rising Anti-Corruption Weekend is a chance to explore how our corrupt system prevents progress on all major issues, encourages political extremism, and concentrates power among the wealthy at the expense of the rest of us. And it’s the chance to ask:

“What am I going to do about it?”

Symposium Speakers
“We need to take our democracy back”
Lawrence Lessig

Professor of Law at Harvard Law School, Director of the Safra Center for Ethics at Harvard University, and political activist, Lawrence Lessig transformed intellectual-property law when he co-founded Creative Commons. It was during his efforts to improve copyright and cyberlaw that he ran headlong into the power of money in Congress. Now Lessig aims to realize a more fundamental transformation: fixing the system of corruption in American politics. He has founded the activist organization Rootstrikers, and launched MayDay PAC, a crowd-funded “super PAC to destroy all super PACs”. His latest book, Republic, Lost, was a major source of inspiration for this weekend.
“Average citizens have little or no independent influence”
Martin Gilens

Professor of Politics at Princeton University, Martin Gilens’ research includes investigating how the wealthy have undue sway on American politics. Along with Benjamin Page of Northwestern University, Gilens authored the recent paper Testing Theories of American Politics: Elites, Interest Groups, and Average Citizens which went viral in the media, being cited as evidence that the U.S. is an oligarchy. Their findings show that average citizens have little or no influence on policy outcomes. When economic elites and business groups disagree with the public majority, legislators pass policy favoring the affluent. This study, along with Gilens’ latest book, Affluence and Influence, was a major factor in motivating this weekend.
